## Introduction

An essential part of interoperability lies in a common understanding of the meaning of exchanged data between the parties involved. Standards that have strong semantics, e.g. standards that mandates clearf description of the objects in the message, are preferred. Ideally, you would want to use one central vocabulary where are defintions reside. However, standards are built around certain domains and thereby cover a limited area. The Smart Grid Architecture Reference Model (SGAM) is specifically designed to map the standards to domains and zones as shown below:




![SGAm layers](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/691c28bc-c408-41b8-8bfa-66eb1a34467b)


Inevitably, the combination of several standards must be applied. Crucial is, that they have strong semantics which allows for a semantic cross reference between the different standards, a task that needs to be performed manually in coordinating with the standardisation experts in the field. Apart from the semantic aspect, the coverage of a standard in a certain domain, can not be ignored. If the implemwentation base is substantial, ignoring it, will cause a large part of targeted stakeholders to either change their back-end systems to use another standard.Most likely, they will refer from participating. One last aspect to consider is whether a standard is official registered by the CEN/CENELEC authority. This marks a standard as European qualified for use. The three criteria can be conflicting, like a world-wide standard with medium semantics or a USA-governed strong semantic standard. Project INSIEME's information model is centered around the EN IEC 62325-351 standard, being the European Style Market Profile of the Common Information Model.
